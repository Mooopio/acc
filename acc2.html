Section 01

Accounting Assumptions & Principles

Two groups: related to the Balance Sheet (B/S) and related to the Income Statement (I/S). Know them cold.

🏢 1. Accounting Entity Assumption

The entity (company, project, enterprise, organization) has a separate legal personality from its owner. The entity is treated as an independent person who owns its own assets and liabilities — totally separate from the owner's personal finances.

Key phrase: "The accounting entity is an independent person." If a question asks why a company's debt is not the owner's personal debt → this assumption!

♾️ 2. Going Concern Assumption

The entity is established to continue indefinitely (its goal is making profit), not to be terminated in a short period. This justifies recording assets at historical cost rather than liquidation value.

Key phrase: "Continuous life." If exam asks why we don't record assets at liquidation price → Going Concern!

💰 3. The Cost Principle (Historical Cost)

Assets and liabilities must be recorded and disclosed at their historical cost — the cost of acquiring the asset at the purchasing date. The B/S amounts do NOT reflect current market values.

Key phrase: "Every amount in the B/S = historical cost." If exam asks why B/S values ≠ market values → Cost Principle!

💵 4. Stable Monetary Unit Assumption

Assumes the monetary unit (Dollar, EGP, etc.) has fixed purchasing power — which is actually incorrect in reality because:

  • 📉 Inflation → purchasing power decreases
  • 📈 Deflation → purchasing power increases
Key phrase: "Fixed purchasing power (unrealistic assumption)." This is the ONE assumption they love to test as a trick question — it's an assumption, not a fact!

📅 1. Periodicity Assumption (Time Period)

Divides the life of the business into equal periods (accounting period, usually a year) to measure net income for each period — instead of waiting until the end of the business's life.

  • Entity may prepare financial statements quarterly, semi-annually, or monthly
  • BUT must prepare them annually at minimum
Key phrase: "Equal periods to measure profit per period." Why do we have annual reports? → Periodicity!

🎯 2. Revenue Recognition Principle

Revenues should be recorded when REALIZED. Revenue is realized when:

  • ✅ Goods are delivered (sold) to customers, OR
  • ✅ Service is rendered (completed)

This happens regardless of cash collection — you don't need to receive cash to record revenue!

Trick: Revenue on credit → still record it NOW when service is done. Cash comes later. Revenue = realized ≠ received.

🤝 3. The Matching Principle

Since expenses are incurred to produce revenues, we must match revenues and expenses in the same period to get the correct net result (net income or loss).

Formula: Revenues − Expenses = Net Income (or Net Loss if negative). Always match them in the same period!

Section 02

The Accounting Equation

The most important equation in accounting. It ALWAYS must stay balanced — every single transaction.

ASSETS = LIABILITIES + OWNER'S EQUITY

Economic Resources = Creditors' Claims + Owners' Claims

🏗️

ASSETS

Economic resources the company OWNS. Split into: Long-term (Land, Building, Equipment, Furniture) and Current (Cash, Inventory, Receivables). Also includes Prepaid Expenses, Intangible Assets (Goodwill, Patent).

💳

LIABILITIES

What the company OWES to outsiders. Long-term: Bonds Payable. Current: Accounts Payable, Loans Payable. Other: Unearned Revenue, Expenses Payable.

👤

OWNER'S EQUITY

= Capital + Additional Investment + Net Income (Revenue − Expenses) − Drawings − Net Loss. It's what belongs to the owner after paying all debts.

⚖️

Always Balanced

EVERY transaction affects at least two items but the equation stays equal. If assets go up → either liabilities go up OR owner's equity goes up by the same amount.

Section 04

The Debit & Credit Rule

This is the core of bookkeeping. Every account has a NORMAL side. Going with it = increase. Against it = decrease.

DEBIT = Left Side  |  CREDIT = Right Side

Comes from Latin: Debit = "he owes" / Credit = "he trusts"

Account Type Normal Balance To INCREASE To DECREASE Examples
Assets DEBIT DEBIT CREDIT Cash, Building, Equipment, A/R, Supplies
Liabilities CREDIT CREDIT DEBIT Loans Payable, A/P, N/P, Unearned Revenue
Owner's Equity / Capital CREDIT CREDIT DEBIT Capital account
Revenue CREDIT CREDIT DEBIT Service Revenue, Fees, Interest Revenue
Expenses DEBIT DEBIT CREDIT Salaries, Rent, Supplies Expense, Interest Expense
Drawings DEBIT DEBIT CREDIT Owner withdrawals for personal use
✅

Assets → DEBIT Normal

Assets live on the LEFT side of the accounting equation, so they have a DEBIT normal balance. Buying more assets = DEBIT. Selling/using assets = CREDIT.

✅

Liabilities → CREDIT Normal

Liabilities are on the RIGHT side of the equation. Taking on more debt = CREDIT. Paying off debt = DEBIT.

✅

Equity/Revenue → CREDIT Normal

Owner's Equity and Revenue both INCREASE equity → CREDIT. Revenue recognition = CREDIT Revenue. Paying revenue out = rare DEBIT.

✅

Expenses/Drawings → DEBIT Normal

Expenses and Drawings DECREASE equity (they're "contra-equity"). More expense = DEBIT Expense. This is why expenses feel like assets in terms of their normal side.

Section 07

🧠 Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

Use these to lock the concepts in your brain forever.

Mnemonic #1 — Normal Balance Sides

"ADE-LER" → Assets, Drawings, Expenses have DEBIT normal balance. Liabilities, Equity, Revenue have CREDIT normal balance.

Trick #2 — Revenue vs Cash

"Earn first, collect later." Revenue recognition = when you DO the work. Cash collection can come later. This is how accrual accounting works. Credit sales → record Revenue NOW, debit A/R. Later when cash arrives → debit Cash, credit A/R.

Trick #3 — Unearned Revenue is a LIABILITY

Think: customer paid you $1000 to do a job you HAVEN'T done yet. That money is not yours yet — you OWE them the service. So it's a liability. Once you do the service → remove the liability, recognize revenue.

Trick #4 — Prepaid vs Payable

Prepaid = ASSET (you paid for something you'll use in the FUTURE — like rent in advance). Payable = LIABILITY (you used something you'll pay for in the FUTURE — like rent not yet paid).

Trick #5 — The Equation Always Balances

If you ever doubt a journal entry or transaction analysis: COUNT THE DEBITS AND CREDITS. Total Debits must ALWAYS = Total Credits. If they don't match, you made an error. No exceptions.

Trick #6 — Drawings vs Expenses

Both reduce equity and have DEBIT normal balance. But: Drawings = owner takes cash for PERSONAL use. Expenses = company pays for BUSINESS use. Don't mix them up on the exam!

Trick #7 — Goodwill & Patent = INTANGIBLE Assets

Intangible = you can't touch them. Goodwill (company reputation value) and Patent (exclusive rights) are on the B/S as ASSETS even though they're invisible. They still appear on the LEFT side of the equation.

Trick #8 — Stable Monetary Unit = The WRONG Assumption

This is the only assumption that is known to be incorrect. The other 3 B/S assumptions are useful simplifications. But Stable Monetary Unit ignores inflation. If exam asks "which assumption is unrealistic?" → Stable Monetary Unit!

Trick #9 — B/S vs I/S: What Goes Where

B/S = Snapshot (one specific date). Contains: Assets, Liabilities, Capital. I/S = Movie (a period of time). Contains: Revenues, Expenses, Net Income/Loss. Net Income from I/S flows INTO the B/S through Owner's Equity.

Trick #10 — Comprehensive Example Pattern (Example 2-3)

For every transaction: (1) What accounts are affected? (2) Do they go up or down? (3) Which side of the equation? Always label: Assets = Liabilities + OE. Check the final totals balance. The pattern never changes.
