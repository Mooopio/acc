Section 01 · Definition of Accounting

What is Accounting?

As a System

The System View

"To provide quantitative information with financial nature, about an economic entity to help in making economic decisions."

Three keywords to remember — use the trick below:

  • 1
    Quantitative Information
    Numbers only. No info about location, staff, hiring = excluded.
  • 2
    Financial Nature
    Must be expressible in monetary units (money). Non-monetary = out.
  • 3
    Decision Making
    The ultimate goal. All accounting exists to help someone decide.
🧠 Memory Trick
"Quiet Financial Decisions"
Q = Quantitative · F = Financial · D = Decisions
As a Process

The Process View

"Identifying, recording, summarizing, and reporting economic information about an economic entity to decision makers."

4 Steps — must know them in ORDER:

  • 1
    Identifying
    Analyze transactions → separate financial from non-financial events.
  • 2
    Recording (Journalizing)
    Write financial events into the journal (the books/records).
  • 3
    Summarizing
    Transfer to ledger, classify accounts, make Trial Balance.
  • 4
    Reporting
    Prepare the general-purpose financial statements.
🧠 Memory Trick
"I Really Study Reports"
I=Identify · R=Record · S=Summarize · R=Report
Section 02 · Accounting Users

Who Uses Accounting?

⚙️ Internal Users

Inside the Company

Need detailed, financial & non-financial, historical & estimated information.

  • Planning the future activities
  • Coordinating between departments
  • Directing day-to-day operations
  • Controlling & taking corrective actions

Plan · Coordinate · Direct · Control
Management Workers / Employees

Outside the Company

Need total, historical, financial information about firm's activities and results.

  • Know about Profitability
  • Know about Resources & their uses
  • Evaluate financial position
  • Evaluate ability to grow, continue & pay debts
Investors Creditors & Banks Suppliers Customers Workers Unions Competitors Government Financial Analysts Public

Section 03 · Accounting Reports

Types of Reports

📋 Internal Reports

Prepared for internal users only (management, inside the firm).

  • Budgets
  • Cost Statements
  • Performance Evaluation Reports
  • Responsibility Accounting Reports

📄 External Reports

Called General-Purpose Financial Statements. For both internal & external, but mainly external.

  • Statement of Financial Position (Balance Sheet)
  • Income Statement
  • Statement of Owners' Equity
  • Statement of Cash Flow

The 4 Financial Statements — Deep Dive

Contains 3 accounts called Permanent / Real accounts. Shows the financial position of the entity.

Assets (Rights / Resources)

Expected economic benefits controlled by the entity as a result of past transactions.

  • Land · Building · Equipment · Furniture
  • Cash (Box + Bank)
  • Inventory
  • Office Supplies
  • Accounts Receivable
  • Notes Receivable
  • Prepaid Expenses
  • Accrued Revenues

Liabilities (Obligations)

Future economic sacrifices resulting from current obligations to transfer or render service in the future.

  • Accounts Payable
  • Notes Payable
  • Expenses Payable
  • Unearned Revenues
  • Loans

Owner's Equity (Capital)

The remaining of assets after subtracting all liabilities.

  • Capital
  • + Net Profit
  • (−) Drawings
The Accounting Equation (MOST IMPORTANT)
Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity
Or: Owner's Equity = Assets − Liabilities

Prepared at the end of each accounting period (usually a year). Contains 2 accounts called Temporary / Nominal accounts: Revenues & Expenses.

Revenues (Credits)

Price for selling products/goods OR rendering services — regardless of when collected.

  • Sales Revenue
  • Interest Revenue
  • Services Revenue

Expenses (Debits)

Cost of providing resources & facilities to perform service or sell products.

  • Insurance Expense
  • Rent Expense
  • Electricity Expense
  • Salaries Expense
Profit (Net Income) = Revenues − Expenses > 0
Loss = Revenues − Expenses < 0

📈 Gains (Increase Equity)

Inflow from non-central / non-repeatable activities of the firm. (e.g. selling old equipment)

📉 Losses as an Item (Decrease Equity)

Expense from central/repeatable activities with no benefits in return. Shown inside income statement.

🧠 Key Distinction — Losses
Loss as an item = inside income statement (it's an expense, central activity, no benefit)
Loss as a result = when total Revenues < total Expenses (the bottom line is negative)

Measures and calculates the company's obligations toward its owners.

Owner's Equity Components

  • Capital (initial investment)
  • + Net Income (if profitable period)
  • − Drawings (money taken out by owner)
  • − Net Loss (if loss period)
Ending OE = Capital + Net Income − Drawings

Determines and calculates the cash in and cash out of the company.

💵 Cash Inflows (Cash Collected / Cash In)

  • Cash collected from customers
  • Cash received from investments
  • Loans received

💸 Cash Outflows (Cash Paid / Cash Out)

  • Cash paid for expenses
  • Cash paid to suppliers
  • Loan repayments
Section 04 · Accounting Objectives

The 4 Objectives

01

Determine Profit or Loss

Profit/Loss = Total Revenues − Total Expenses

Tool used: Income Statement

02

Determine Assets & Liabilities

Know the rights (resources) and obligations of the enterprise.

Tool used: Statement of Financial Position

03

Determine Cash Flows

Cash inflows (collected) and cash outflows (paid) of the enterprise.

Tool used: Cash Flows Statement

04

Determine Owner's Rights

Rights of the owners of the firm (owner's equity).

Tool used: Statement of Owners' Equity

Section 05 · Fields of Accounting

9 Fields of Accounting

📊Financial Accounting
▼
Provides information for external users through analyzing, recording, classifying, summarizing and reporting financial events.

→ Think: General-purpose financial statements for the outside world.
🎯Managerial Accounting
▼
Provides information for internal users to help in planning and controlling activities.

Tools used: Budgets · Responsibility Accounting · Cost Accounting
Internal Only Planning Controlling
💲Cost Accounting
▼
Provides info for internal users. Records, measures and reports cost information to help in:

1. Costing products and services
2. Controlling cost

Uses: Actual cost system OR Predetermined cost system

If Predetermined system → compare actual vs. predetermined → find variances → know reasons → take corrective action.
Actual Cost System Predetermined Cost System Variances
📅Budgeting
▼
A system of Planning and Controlling:

Planning: Translating the company's goals into numerical expression in the form of a certified plan.

Controlling: Comparing actual results with the planned ones → evaluate performance → take corrective actions if needed.
Planning Controlling Numerical Goals Corrective Actions
🏛️Governmental Accounting
▼
Deals with governmental activities and units at all levels.

1. Preparing the public budget and final accounts → know financial positions.
2. Doing financial control before and after spending.
📋Tax Accounting
▼
Helps in:
1. Determining taxable income.
2. Determining the amount of taxes to be paid.
3. Tax planning = trying to reduce taxes to the minimum / maximize use of exemptions or tax credits.
🌍National Accounting
▼
Provides information for the state (government). Accounts for all sectors of the national economy by:

1. Measuring the national income and each sector's contribution.
2. Preparing the national budget, national income accounts, and input/output tables.
✈️International Accounting
▼
Deals with:
1. Accounting issues of foreign currencies and exchange rates.
2. Preparing financial statements for multinational companies.
3. Translation of financial statements of foreign branches and companies.
4. Harmonizing local and international accounting standards.
5. Reducing differences in practices between nations to keep comparability.
Foreign Currencies Multinational Harmonization Comparability
🔍Auditing
▼
Examining the whole company by:
1. Examine and evaluate internal control system.
2. Review documents, records and financial statements.

Purpose: Collect evidence from inside and outside the company, so the auditor can give his professional opinion on the fairness of the financial statements. The auditor is responsible for any negligence in his audit.
📌Responsibility Accounting
▼
Provides information for internal users (management).

Means dividing the company into units called Responsibility Centers.

• Every center has a manager.
• That manager is responsible for all revenues and expenses under his control and authority only.
• Manager's performance is evaluated by variances resulting from their decisions.
Responsibility Centers Manager Accountability Variance Analysis

Section 06 · Legal Forms of Business

3 Legal Business Forms

01

Proprietorship

Solo. One owner. Simplest form.

  • ONE owner only
  • Owner gets all profit OR takes all loss
  • Owner IS the manager
  • Unlimited personal liability
02

Partnership

Team. 2–20 partners.

  • More than one owner (partner)
  • Max 20 partners
  • Each unlimited partner = a manager
  • Shared profit / loss
03

Corporation

The big one. Unlimited owners (shareholders).

  • Unlimited number of shareholders
  • Huge capital
  • LIMITED liability (per shares owned)
  • Legally SEPARATE entity from owners
  • Unlimited ability to raise funds
  • Flexibility of capital transfer
  • Continuity
  • Separate legal entity
🧠 Corporation's 4 Characteristics — UFCS
"United Families Create Separation"
U=Unlimited ability to raise funds · F=Flexibility of capital transfer · C=Continuity · S=Separate legal entity
Section 07 · Key Terms Glossary

Master the Vocabulary

Assets
Expected economic benefits controlled by the entity as a result of past transactions.
Liabilities
Future economic sacrifices resulting from current obligations to transfer or render service to other entities in the future as a result of past transactions or events.
Owner's Equity
The remaining of assets after subtracting all liabilities. Also called Capital.
Revenue
Price for selling products/goods OR rendering services, regardless of whether collected during the period or not.
Expenses
Cost of providing resources and facilities necessary to perform service or sell products.
Profit (Net Income)
Remaining revenues after subtracting all expenses. Revenues > Expenses. Increases equity.
Loss (as a result)
When Revenues < Expenses. The bottom line is negative. Decreases equity.
Gains
Inflow from non-central / non-repeatable activities. Increases equity.
Loss (as an item)
Expense from central / repeatable activities with no benefits in return. Goes inside income statement. Decreases equity.
Permanent Accounts
Assets, Liabilities, Owner's Equity. Found in Balance Sheet. Carry over between periods.
Temporary/Nominal Accounts
Revenues & Expenses. Found in Income Statement. Reset to zero at end of each period.
Responsibility Centers
Units that a company is divided into in Responsibility Accounting. Each has a manager accountable only for items under their control.
